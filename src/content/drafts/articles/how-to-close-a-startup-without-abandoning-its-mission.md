For three years, [Communiti Labs](/projects/communitilabs) pursued a simple mission: help ensure that no community voice was lost.

We built products and assembled a team around it. Customers, pilot partners, investors and advisers chose to support it. The mission became part of how I described the company and part of how I understood myself.

Then I had to make the decision to wind the company down.

The commercial reasons matter. We built something useful, but we did not establish a repeatable and sustainable path to market quickly enough. The personal reasons matter too. My health had reached a point where continuing to carry the company would have asked more of me—and of the people around me—than I could responsibly keep giving.

Once the decision was made, a different question appeared:

> How do you close the company without treating the mission as if it failed a shareholder vote and disappeared with it?

I'm still learning the answer. This isn't a legal or insolvency checklist; accountants, lawyers, directors and regulators have important roles in a wind-down. It's the stewardship problem that sits beside that work.

## The company is a vehicle

Founders are encouraged to speak as if the company and the mission are inseparable.

“We exist to…”

“Our purpose is…”

“We will not stop until…”

That language can be powerful. It aligns a team and makes an abstract future feel like something we can work on today. It can also make an ending feel morally impossible.

If the company is the mission, closing the company means betraying the cause. Continuing becomes evidence of conviction, no matter the cost. Stopping becomes weakness.

I don't think that's a healthy way to understand a mission-led startup.

The company is a vehicle: a particular group of people, products, contracts, capital and assumptions assembled to move a problem in a better direction. The mission is the change the vehicle exists to serve.

A vehicle can become commercially unsustainable. Its route can be wrong. Its driver can become unable to continue. A better vehicle may appear. None of those facts makes the destination less worthwhile.

Separating the two isn't a rhetorical trick to make failure feel nicer. It creates the possibility of ending one set of obligations responsibly while preserving what is still useful to others.

## Responsibility comes before legacy

When a company is closing, it is tempting to begin with the public story.

There is an announcement to write, years of work to explain and a final opportunity to shape how the company will be remembered. That work matters. It shouldn't be first.

The people directly affected deserve to hear from you before the audience does.

For us, that meant speaking privately with the organisations that had an operational relationship with Communiti Labs. Affected customers were refunded. Access, exports and data handling were managed directly. Organisations involved in active opportunities were contacted rather than left to discover the news in a public post.

The same principle applies to the team, co-founders, investors, suppliers and anyone with a real dependency on the company.

The sequence should be:

1. understand the obligations;
2. protect the people affected;
3. create practical certainty where you can;
4. only then tell the broader story.

A mission isn't preserved by a beautiful farewell that leaves someone else carrying an avoidable mess.

## Run two tracks

I found it useful to think of the final work as two related tracks.

The first is the **wind-down track**:

- customers and support;
- team and contractors;
- contracts and suppliers;
- privacy, data and access;
- financial and legal obligations;
- infrastructure, domains and accounts;
- documentation and records.

The second is the **continuity track**:

- technology and intellectual property;
- product knowledge and research;
- customer outcomes and evidence;
- potential acquirers, licensees or stewards;
- a realistic transition plan;
- the public record of what was attempted and learned.

The continuity track can't be allowed to delay the first one indefinitely. A possible transaction isn't a plan for meeting today's obligations. Hope is valuable; dependency on an unsigned deal is dangerous.

Running both tracks makes the boundary visible. We can look for a responsible future for the work without keeping customers, the team or my health suspended while that search plays out.

## Inventory the mission, not only the assets

A traditional deal room is organised around assets and risk: source code, contracts, financials, cap tables, intellectual property, customers, infrastructure and technical architecture.

Those are necessary. They don't fully describe what might be worth carrying forward.

The mission also lives in:

- the problem definition refined through hundreds of conversations;
- the product principles that survived contact with real work;
- the mistakes that eliminated attractive but wrong paths;
- the evidence that a particular workflow created value;
- the trust and language developed with an industry;
- the people who now understand the problem more deeply;
- the unfinished hypotheses that another team could test.

At Communiti Labs, one of those principles was that it should never be good enough to say, “the platform told me so”. Every insight about community feedback needed to lead back to the original evidence. Another was that participation should form a connected loop from planning and collection through analysis, decision-making and follow-up.

Even if none of the original code survives, those principles can.

So a responsible closure should leave more than a repository and a folder of corporate documents. It should leave a map: what we believed, what we tested, what we learned, what worked, what failed and what remains unresolved.

## Find a home, not simply a buyer

Before moving to a complete shutdown, we began a small number of strategic conversations with organisations that might have the capacity to carry parts of the work forward.

That could mean:

- an acquisition of the company or its assets;
- an intellectual-property purchase;
- a licence;
- a product absorbed into an existing platform;
- a team or founder joining another organisation;
- a research or knowledge transfer;
- selective open sourcing;
- a successor independently rebuilding the thesis.

The highest price is not automatically the best home for a mission.

I would want to ask:

- Does this organisation understand the people the work is intended to serve?
- Does it have the capacity to maintain and support the product?
- Will it respect the trust, privacy and evidence standards built into the work?
- Is the mission central enough to survive the next change of priorities?
- Can customers and partners be transitioned without creating new risk?
- Are we transferring something useful, or merely postponing its deletion?

Those questions don't remove the need for commercial reality. A transaction has to be viable for both sides. They do help distinguish continuity from a logo quietly added to someone else's product page.

There's also a duty not to promise an ending that doesn't yet exist. During a process like this, the honest language is: **we are exploring whether the work can continue, but there is nothing definitive to announce.**

Hope should not be confused with certainty.

## Time-box the rescue attempt

A mission can make it very difficult to stop searching for one more option.

One more potential acquirer. One more introduction. One more structure that might keep the technology alive. One more month of personal runway.

That can turn a careful wind-down into a second, quieter form of founder burnout.

The continuity process needs boundaries:

- a defined list of credible parties;
- a clear package of information for each stage;
- a deadline for expressions of serious interest;
- a minimum standard for mission, customer and technical fit;
- limits on exclusivity and unpaid transition work;
- a point at which unresolved possibilities stop delaying closure.

Time-boxing isn't an admission that the mission matters less. It recognises that a founder's health and a team's certainty matter too.

If a mission can survive only while one exhausted person refuses to let go, it has not become an institution. It has become a dependency.

## Open source is not a funeral rite

There is a common suggestion when a software company closes: open source everything.

Sometimes that is exactly right. It can give a community control, allow useful components to continue and preserve knowledge that would otherwise disappear.

It isn't automatically responsible.

The code may contain dependencies, secrets, customer-specific assumptions or security risks that require careful removal. The product may be impossible to operate without infrastructure and knowledge that aren't documented. An open repository may create expectations of maintenance no one has agreed to provide. Intellectual property may also be the company's best chance of returning value to creditors or shareholders, or of enabling a more capable steward to continue the product.

“We published the code” isn't the same as “the mission continues”.

Open source should be evaluated as one transition model among several. It needs an intended community, a usable release, clear ownership and an honest statement about support. Otherwise it may be less an act of preservation than a public archive of abandonment.

## Tell the truth without writing an indictment

A closing announcement has to hold several truths at once.

Communiti Labs did not become the sustainable company we intended to build.

It also did real and consequential work. In 2025, Communiti Analysis processed more than 2.5 million community contributions and returned thousands of hours to engagement teams. A small group of people built technically difficult products, earned customer trust and helped practitioners complete work that mattered.

Product impact is not commercial sustainability. Commercial failure does not retroactively make the impact imaginary.

I think a useful public record should include:

- the question that started the company;
- what was actually built;
- the people who made it possible;
- concrete evidence of the value created;
- the commercial and personal truth behind the decision;
- what the founder would do differently;
- what is known and unknown about the future of the work;
- what the industry might carry forward.

It shouldn't turn the market into a villain. It shouldn't imply that customers owed the startup their risk, budget or advocacy. It shouldn't disguise failure behind the language of “graduating”, “sunsetting” or “an exciting new chapter” if that language avoids the truth.

Honesty is more useful to the mission than mythology.

## Protect the people from the outcome

One sentence mattered enough to include in our public account:

> The outcome of the company should never be mistaken for a reflection of the quality, commitment or capability of the people who built it.

Startups routinely borrow the team's identity to tell a story of collective momentum. At the end, the founder has a responsibility to return that identity intact.

Name what people achieved. Make introductions. Provide references. Preserve artefacts they can show where appropriate. Pay what is owed. Be clear about the decisions that belonged to leadership rather than allowing ambiguity to attach itself to the team.

The mission does not survive if the people who carried it leave believing their work was wasted.

They may carry the problem, the principles and the experience into dozens of future organisations. That may ultimately be a larger legacy than the original company could have created alone.

## Preserve the evidence

A mission-led startup shouldn't leave only a triumphant case study or a post-mortem written entirely in regret. It should leave an evidence trail.

For Communiti Labs, that means keeping an honest account of:

- the discovery that led us to the problem;
- the progression from Communiti Analysis to Communiti Conversations;
- the customer outcomes and impact estimates;
- the product and technical principles;
- the gap between enthusiasm and willingness or capacity to buy;
- the go-to-market assumptions I would challenge earlier;
- the role of procurement, change management and category creation;
- the features and hypotheses we did not have time to complete.

Some of that now lives in the [Communiti Labs project record](/projects/communitilabs). Some belongs in private handover documents. Some may become future articles or talks.

The point isn't to make the closure productive enough to justify the pain. It's to prevent future people—including future me—from having to relearn every lesson from the beginning.

## The founder is not an acceptable sacrifice

Mission-led work is particularly good at making unsustainable behaviour feel virtuous.

There is always another customer to help, another product gap to close, another funding conversation to prepare for and another person whose belief you do not want to disappoint. When the problem is socially important, rest can feel like time stolen from people who need the work.

That logic has no natural stopping point.

My health becoming part of the decision does not mean the mission failed to inspire enough commitment. It means commitment without capacity eventually stops being stewardship.

Closing can be the responsible act. It can protect customers from a company that can no longer support them properly, return certainty to a team, prevent more capital and health being consumed by an invalid assumption, and create space for the mission to find another form.

The founder matters independently of the company. That should be obvious. Startup culture has a remarkable ability to make it sound controversial.

## What it means for the mission to continue

I don't know whether Communiti Analysis or Communiti Conversations will continue as products. At the time of writing, discussions about the technology and intellectual property are still a possibility rather than an outcome.

I do know the need remains.

Community voices still deserve to be heard, understood and reflected in the decisions that shape people's lives. Engagement professionals still deserve tools that give them more time for relationships and judgement. Participants still deserve to know what was heard, what changed, what did not change and why.

That work does not belong to Communiti Labs simply because we tried to build a company around it.

The mission continues when another founder learns from what we got wrong, when an established provider adopts a better evidence standard or when a practitioner closes the feedback loop even though their software doesn't make it easy. It continues when someone from our team carries the principles into a new product. It also continues if a better approach replaces our technology completely.

Closing a startup without abandoning its mission is not about keeping the company alive in disguise.

It is about meeting the obligations of the ending, preserving the truth of the work, giving useful things the best chance of continuing and then accepting that the mission was never ours to own.
