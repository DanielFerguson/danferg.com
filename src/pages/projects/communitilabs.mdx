## The outcome, honestly

Communiti Labs did not become the sustainable company we set out to build. It also did real and consequential work.

Across three years, our team moved from research interviews and rough sketches to products that helped engagement practitioners bring fragmented community feedback into one evidence trail. In 2025 alone, Communiti Analysis processed more than 2.5 million contributions and returned thousands of hours to the people responsible for understanding them.

Those truths belong together. Product impact is not the same thing as commercial sustainability, and a meaningful mission does not remove the need for a repeatable path to market. This is the story of what we built, how it evolved, what worked, and what I would do differently.

## The question that started it

Steven Germain and I began with a broad question: **how might we help Victorians better engage with community and natural spaces?** We did not begin with a predetermined product.

We cold-called and emailed around 100 practitioners and asked what they loved about their work and what stopped them from doing it well. Across those conversations we identified roughly 13 recurring challenges. One thread connected almost everyone: they had too much data and not enough time, resources, or specialised skills to bring it together meaningfully.

These were _people people_. They cared about advocating for communities, building relationships, and helping organisations make better decisions. But the better they engaged, the more work they created for themselves across workshops, surveys, handwritten notes, spreadsheets, submissions, recordings, interviews, and reports.

Our ambition became simple to say and difficult to deliver: **help ensure that no community voice was lost.**

## Turning research into product rules

We began with Communiti Analysis and spent 2024 co-designing it with practitioners and organisations across 13 local-government areas and councils. That was separate from the 13 recurring challenges identified during discovery: one number described the research themes; the other described the breadth of people and places involved in shaping the product.

The work established a set of non-negotiable product rules:

- Accept the messy formats engagement actually produces.
- Preserve the original evidence behind every conclusion.
- Keep practitioners in control of interpretation and review.
- Make the final reasoning easy to trace, question, and defend.

The first prototype proved the broad workflow. Twelve months of observation and feedback turned it into a shared analysis workspace grounded in how projects were actually run.

## Taking Communiti Analysis to market

The first public version was presented at Engage by the Beach in 2024. In 2025, we took the product to market as Communiti Analysis.

It brought surveys, spreadsheets, documents, written submissions, transcripts, recordings, images, and in-person feedback into one workspace. Teams could identify themes, topics, sentiment, arguments, and supporting evidence without treating every channel as a separate analysis project.

## Auditability was the product

The aim was never merely to produce a plausible summary or add a chatbot to an existing workflow. Every answer needed to show its working.

Themes, arguments, positions, and reports had to lead reviewers back to the individual, verbatim contributions from which they were derived. The technology was intended to support practitioner judgement, not quietly replace it. That distinction mattered in work shaped by public accountability, conflicting experiences, and decisions with real consequences.

> It is never good enough to say, “The platform told me so.”

Building that evidence trail at the convergence of rapidly developing language models, traditional machine learning, and qualitative and quantitative research was one of the hardest technical challenges of my career. It was also one of the most rewarding.

## Looking beyond the online portal

Our research eventually led to a broader question: **what would community engagement look like if technology supported the entire process, not only the survey or the analysis at the end?**

That question became Communiti Conversations. We began building an engagement operating system through which teams could plan together, engage across multiple channels, review participation consistently, hand feedback into analysis, and close the loop with their communities.

Communities should not have to come to a platform simply because it is convenient for the organisation running the engagement. Participation needed to work across web forms, SMS, email, voice, uploads, in-person activities, and the other channels people already used. Multilingual participation and follow-up also needed to be part of the complete workflow rather than separate, inconsistent tasks.

We did not have enough time to give Conversations the future it deserved. Its first pilots nevertheless strengthened my belief that the underlying thesis was right: public participation needs connected systems for planning, participation, analysis, decision-making, and accountability—not simply another online portal.

## What did not work

Meaningful product outcomes did not become a repeatable and sustainable path to market quickly enough. Enthusiasm for the mission was real, but enthusiasm and the organisational conditions required to purchase, adopt, and champion a new category of technology were not the same thing.

We could have narrowed our focus sooner. I could have validated willingness and capacity to buy more rigorously, treated procurement and change management as part of the product problem, and built a more disciplined go-to-market motion before broadening the platform vision.

Health and personal capacity also shaped the decision to wind down. A company cannot sustainably pursue its mission when continuing requires more from its people than they can responsibly keep giving. As co-founder and CEO, the responsibility for recognising and responding to those realities was ultimately mine.

Failure to create a sustainable company does not erase what the team built or the value customers received. It does change how I understand the work: a strong product, a necessary mission, and impressive use are all meaningful, but none is a substitute for a durable business model.

## What remains

Communiti Labs existed because Steven, Finn, Jarryd, Laura, Becky, our customers, pilot partners, investors, advisers, and many generous practitioners chose to give an ambitious idea their time, trust, criticism, and care. The quality of their work should never be confused with the outcome of the company.

The mission remains important. Community voices still deserve to be heard, understood, and reflected in the decisions that shape people’s lives. The products and technology may yet continue in another form, but their future was not settled when we announced the wind-down.

The full personal account—including the people who made the journey possible and the circumstances behind the decision—is in [Closing a chapter: Thank you from Communiti Labs](https://communitilabs.com/blogs/2026-07-13-closing-a-chapter-thank-you-from-communiti-labs).
